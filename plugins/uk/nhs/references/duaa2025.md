# Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 — clinical safety reference

> Generation guidance for `/arckit:uk-nhs-dcb0129` and `/arckit:uk-nhs-dcb0160`.
> Not legal advice. Verify against the source before relying on it.

## Instrument

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (c. 18) amends the Health and Social Care Act 2012 Part 9 information-standards framework. The health-IT provisions are **section 121** and **Schedule 15** ("Information standards for health and adult social care in England").

- Act contents: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/contents/enacted>
- Section 121: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/section/121/enacted>
- Schedule 15: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/schedule/15/enacted>

## Effect in brief

The amended framework makes explicit that a health or adult social care information standard can apply to information technology itself (computers and other devices, software, and code) and to IT services (development, making available, operation, maintenance). A standard may cover functionality, connectivity, interoperability, portability, storage and access to information, and information security. The framework introduces a "relevant IT provider" concept: a provider suspected of non-compliance may be required to comply, to evidence compliance, or to take specified steps, with a public-censure route and provision for accreditation schemes.

For clinical safety this means DCB0129 and DCB0160 evidence increasingly sits alongside wider information-standard evidence. The Clinical Safety Officer does not own every adjacent domain, but the safety case should state where clinical safety ends and adjacent domains begin, and cross-reference them where a failure could cause patient harm.

## Screening questions

Use the answers to populate the "Applicable standards and assurance domains" and "Roles and responsibilities" tables in `SAFETY.md`.

1. Does the product or service involve health care or adult social care in, or in relation to, England?
2. Is the organisation marketing, supplying, providing, operating, maintaining, or making available IT or an IT service?
3. Could applicable information standards cover functionality, connectivity, interoperability, portability, storage, access, or information security?
4. Could a failure in any of those domains plausibly contribute to patient harm?
5. Could an evidence pack be produced quickly in response to a compliance query?
6. Are owners named for clinical safety, information governance, security, interoperability, operations, and supplier/deployer obligations?
7. Are accreditation or future conformance schemes relevant or pending?
